Week 9 - Questions

2015/7/26

So far, all of my questions have been so basic that I could find the answer with about five minutes of Google research. In this post, I will share what I've learned about finding the information I need on the web.

Ruby-doc - the official and best resource for Ruby information. I had to gain a fair bit of familiarity with Ruby before I found it really useful, but now it gives me exactly the information I need–what a method does, what arguments it takes, and what exactly it spits out.

w3schools - great resource for information that can be easily referenced, for example–valid CSS attributes, or whether that jQuery method was getElementBy... or getElementsBy... They also offer several tutorials, a sandbox for testing code, and a few other resources, but it's greatest value is as a kind of quick-reference cheat-sheet.

Javascript - Javascript is one of the most widely-used languages in the world, and there are no shortage of resources, tutorials and references. The best reference is Mozilla's, though it can be even more dense and intimidating than ruby-docs. W3 Schools has a pretty good Javascript reference library as well, and it's considerably easier to read.

Google - Google search tips are well-documented, and because I enjoy browsing blogs and learning about them, here are a few that taught me something new just now:

  • Google's own help page is surprisingly easy to overlook, but there's a lot of useful information here, particularly under the "Filter and refine your results" section.
  • Here's a fantastic guide for power users from MIT Did you know that including the '~' operator in a Google search will include all synonyms of a word in a search? For example, "Ruby ~tutorial" will return results for ruby tutorial, ruby guide, ruby help, etc... This site is pretty thorough.
